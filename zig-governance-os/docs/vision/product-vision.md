# Product Vision — Zig

## Why Zig exists

Governance, risk, and compliance work today runs on spreadsheets, static frameworks PDFs,
and audit-season scrambles. Teams know they need an asset inventory, a risk register, a
control library, and evidence on file — but the tools available either digitize a
checklist (so the work is still entirely manual) or bury the program inside a heavyweight
GRC suite built for enterprises with a dedicated GRC department.

Zig exists to make governance a living, AI-assisted system instead of a static checklist.
Instead of a blank spreadsheet, a new program starts with an AI-generated baseline tailored
to the organization. Instead of a once-a-year audit scramble, the system continuously
tracks coverage and tells you exactly what's missing and what to do about it. Instead of a
static PDF mapped by hand, framework requirements are metadata that travels with every
asset, risk, and control automatically.

## Who it serves

Zig is built for the people who actually do governance work day to day, not just the
auditors who review it once a year:

- **GRC managers and analysts** at small-to-mid-size companies who are the entire
  governance function, or close to it.
- **Compliance analysts** at organizations pursuing a first SOC 2, ISO 27001, or HIPAA
  attestation and need a structured starting point.
- **Independent consultants** who run governance programs for multiple client
  organizations and need to move fast across all of them.
- **Founders and small-business owners** who need to stand up a credible governance
  posture before a deal, a customer security review, or a fundraise — without hiring a
  full GRC team first.
- **Auditors** who need a clear, evidence-backed trail to review rather than a folder of
  loose documents.

## What problems it solves

1. **The blank-page problem.** Most governance programs die at the starting line because
   nobody knows what their first risk register or control library should even contain. Zig
   generates a complete, framework-aware starting program from a short guided intake.
2. **The disconnected-spreadsheet problem.** Assets, risks, controls, and evidence usually
   live in separate files with no real relationship between them, so nobody can answer
   "which controls actually cover this asset, and where's the evidence." Zig enforces one
   connected model: Asset → Risk → Control → Framework Requirement → Evidence → Task →
   Report.
3. **The opaque-maturity problem.** Teams often can't say how ready they are for an audit
   until the audit happens. Zig's governance score is explainable at all times: it states
   what it's measuring, why the number is what it is, and exactly what action would move it.
4. **The static-framework problem.** Mapping a control library to ISO 27001, SOC 2, NIST
   CSF, CIS Controls, HIPAA, and PCI DSS by hand is slow and error-prone, and has to be
   redone whenever a framework updates. In Zig, frameworks are metadata attached to the
   same underlying records, so one control can show its coverage across every framework it
   touches simultaneously.

## Why it's different

Zig is not a checklist app with an AI chat bolted on. The AI Command Center is a governance
operator: it generates programs, risks, controls, and framework mappings directly into the
same connected data model the rest of the product uses, and every recommendation it makes
states its reasoning, its supporting data, and its confidence. The Health Advisor runs
continuously in the background, the way a security monitoring tool would, rather than only
producing a result when someone manually runs a report. And because every module
(Mission Control, Assets, Risks, Controls, Evidence, Tasks, AI Command, Health Advisor,
Reporting) sits on the same Universal Governance Model, there are no dead-end screens and
no orphaned records — every entity in the system traces a path from "why does this exist"
to "what report does this eventually support."
